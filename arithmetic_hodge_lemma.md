# The Arithmetic Hodge Index Lemma
## Formal Statement, Motivation, and Proof Assistant Directive

## Part I: The Lemma

### 1.1 Informal Statement

The natural intersection pairing on the Arakelov-compactified arithmetic surface
Spec(ℤ), restricted to degree-zero classes, is negative-definite. Equivalently: for
any metrized line bundle L on Spec(ℤ) with arithmetic degree zero, the arithmetic
self-intersection number satisfies L̂ · L̂ ≤ 0, with equality if and only if L is
torsion.

(Sign convention: in Arakelov geometry the Hodge Index Theorem gives
negative-definiteness on the orthogonal complement of the ample class, not
positive-definiteness. The content is identical — the pairing is definite, which
forces spectral constraints. We will state the equivalent positivity form below.)

### 1.2 Equivalent Positivity Form (Weil Criterion)

For all Schwartz functions f on ℝ satisfying f = g ∗ g̃ (i.e., f is an
autocorrelation), the Weil functional

    W(f) = f̂(0) + f̂(1)
           − Σ_p Σ_{m=1}^∞ (log p / p^{m/2}) · [f(m log p) + f(−m log p)]
           + (1/2π) ∫_{−∞}^{∞} f̂(t) · [Ψ'(1/4 + it/2) / Ψ(1/4 + it/2)] dt

is non-negative: W(f) ≥ 0.

This is Weil's criterion (1952). Bombieri and Connes independently showed
it is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis.

### 1.3 Decomposed Form (The Three Forces)

The Weil functional decomposes as:

    W(f) = W_polar(f) + W_arch(f) + W_primes(f)

where:

    W_polar(f)  = f̂(0) + f̂(1)                                    [BLANKET]
    W_arch(f)   = (1/2π) ∫ f̂(t) Ω(t) dt                          [ADDITIVE]
    W_primes(f) = −Σ_p Σ_m (log p / p^{m/2}) f(m log p) − (same, −m)  [MULTIPLICATIVE]

The claim: W(f) ≥ 0 for all autocorrelations f = g ∗ g̃.

### 1.4 The Chain of Equivalences

The following are equivalent:

(a) The Riemann Hypothesis (all nontrivial zeros of ζ on Re(s) = 1/2).
(b) The Weil positivity criterion W(g ∗ g̃) ≥ 0 for all admissible g.
(c) The Weil functional W defines a positive-definite distribution.
(d) There exists a Hilbert space H and a self-adjoint operator D on H whose
    spectrum is {γ : ζ(1/2 + iγ) = 0}.
(e) The arithmetic intersection pairing on ĈH¹(Spec(ℤ̄)) is definite on
    the degree-zero subspace.

Equivalences (a)⟺(b) is Weil (1952). (b)⟺(c) is standard functional analysis.
(c)⟺(d) is the GNS construction. (d)⟺(a) is Hilbert-Pólya. (a)⟺(e) is the
content of the Arithmetic Hodge Index program (Connes, Faltings, Arakelov).

## Part II: Why It Should Hold

### 2.1 The Structural Argument

The integers ℤ have three properties that are individually common but collectively
rare:

(P1) ℤ is a principal ideal domain: every ideal is generated by a single element.
     Equivalently, the class number h(ℤ) = 1. There is no "hidden" multiplicative
     structure — every divisibility relation is visible at the element level.

(P2) ℤ is a Euclidean domain: there is a division algorithm. This gives
     the interplay of addition and multiplication a computational form — you can
     always "divide with remainder," which is distribution made algorithmic.

(P3) The additive group (ℤ, +) is self-dual under Pontryagin duality.
     This gives the functional equation of ζ, hence the symmetry axis at 1/2.

In the function field analogue — F_q[t] over a finite field — all three
properties hold, and RH is PROVED (Weil 1948, Deligne 1974). The proof
mechanism is:

    F_q[t] is a PID with self-dual additive structure
    → the associated algebraic curve is smooth and projective
    → the intersection pairing on its cohomology satisfies the Hodge Index Theorem
    → the Frobenius eigenvalues lie on the critical circle
    → RH for function fields. ∎

For ℤ, the identical chain should hold with "algebraic curve over F_q" replaced
by "arithmetic curve" (Spec(ℤ) compactified à la Arakelov), and "Frobenius" replaced
by "scaling flow on the adèle class space."

### 2.2 The Physical Argument (Detailed Balance)

In the Free Energy Principle framework:

- A system at steady state with a Markov blanket satisfies detailed balance.
- Detailed balance ⟹ the transition operator is self-adjoint in the natural
  (stationary-distribution-weighted) inner product.
- Self-adjointness ⟹ real spectrum.
- Real spectrum + the functional equation ⟹ spectrum on the critical line.

For (ℤ, +, ×):

- The Markov blanket is the distributive law (sole coupling of + and ×).
- The distributive law is perfect: a(b+c) = ab + ac holds universally,
  without defect, in both directions.
- Perfect coupling → no information leak across the blanket → detailed balance.
- Detailed balance → the scaling flow on 𝔸_ℚ/ℚ* is unitary → the generator
  is self-adjoint → the spectrum (= zeta zeros) is real → RH.

## Part III: What Breaks If It Doesn't Hold

If the lemma is false — if the arithmetic intersection pairing is indefinite —
then there exists a class α ∈ ĈH¹₀(Spec(ℤ̄)) with α · α > 0 (wrong sign).

This would mean:

(A) A SPECIFIC COMPUTABLE MODE exists where the prime distribution can
    fluctuate at zero cost. The mode is determined by α and could in principle
    be extracted from the proof of indefiniteness.

(B) The Weil functional W is negative on some specific autocorrelation g ∗ g̃.
    The function g would encode the "free fluctuation" — the perturbation that
    doesn't increase surprisal.

(C) There exists a pair of zeta zeros off the critical line, symmetric about it.
    The pair corresponds to the indefinite direction of the pairing.

(D) The distributive law, while algebraically perfect, fails to enforce analytic
    balance at some specific scale. There would exist a frequency at which
    the additive and multiplicative structures of ℤ have an asymmetric
    interaction that the distributive law cannot compensate.

(E) The analogy between ℤ and F_q[t] would break at a precise, identifiable
    point: the arithmetic curve Spec(ℤ̄) would fail to be "smooth" in the
    Arakelov sense, despite ℤ being a PID. This would imply that the
    archimedean place introduces a defect that has no analogue in the
    function field setting.

Finding (E) would itself be a major result — it would identify a fundamental
structural difference between number fields and function fields that current
mathematics does not predict.

## Part IV: The Physical Principle

### Detailed Balance

A Markov process with transition kernel K and stationary distribution π
satisfies detailed balance if:

    π(i) K(i → j) = π(j) K(j → i)    for all states i, j.

This is equivalent to:

    The operator T defined by (Tf)(i) = Σ_j K(i → j) f(j) is self-adjoint
    in the inner product ⟨f, g⟩_π = Σ_i π(i) f(i) ḡ(i).

For the arithmetic system:

- States: the adèlic points of ℚ, modulo ℚ* (the adèle class space X).
- Transition kernel: the scaling flow σ_t on X, which multiplies by e^t
  in the archimedean component.
- Stationary distribution: the Haar measure μ on X (which exists because
  X is a locally compact group quotient).
- Detailed balance: σ_t preserves μ, i.e., μ is σ_t-invariant.

The invariance of Haar measure under the group action is guaranteed by the
algebraic structure — specifically, by the fact that ℚ_p is a field for each p
(which requires perfect distribution) and ℝ is a field (same reason).

The claim: this Haar-measure invariance, which is a consequence of perfect
distribution, implies the unitarity of σ_t on L²(X, μ), which implies
self-adjointness of the generator, which implies spectral reality, which
(combined with the functional equation) implies RH.

The gap: the passage from local invariance (at each place) to global unitarity
(on the quotient X = 𝔸_ℚ/ℚ*) requires controlling the divergences that arise
from the quotient. The quotient by ℚ* introduces identifications that make the
global Haar measure infinite on certain subsets, requiring regularization.
The regularization must preserve unitarity. This is where Connes' program stalls.

#### Gap A: Stone's Theorem for Unbounded Operators

Mathlib has spectral theory for bounded self-adjoint operators. The generator
of the scaling flow is unbounded. Stone's theorem (1932) says: every strongly
continuous one-parameter unitary group on a Hilbert space has a unique
self-adjoint generator.

This is a fundamental result in functional analysis that belongs in Mathlib
regardless of this project. It requires:

- Unbounded operator API (densely defined, closable, closed operators)
- Domains and graph norms
- The Cayley transform (mapping unbounded self-adjoint to bounded unitary)
- The spectral theorem for unbounded self-adjoint operators

Reference: Reed & Simon, "Functional Analysis" (Vol. I), Chapter VIII.

- The Arakelov intersection pairing
- The Arakelov degree map

Reference: Soulé, "Lectures on Arakelov Geometry" (Cambridge, 1992).

This is a LARGE construction project. A minimal viable version for Spec(ℤ)
(which is 1-dimensional, so the intersection theory simplifies) should be
achievable.

### 5.4 Execution Order

Phase 1 (Infrastructure — no new math, pure formalization):
  1. Stone's theorem for unbounded operators
  2. Poisson summation formula
  3. Schwartz space and Fourier transform refinements
  4. Product formula for ℚ (if not already in Mathlib's adele ring theory)

  3. Haar measure invariance from product formula
  4. Unitarity of scaling flow

Phase 5 (Arakelov geometry — heavy construction):
  1. Metrized line bundles on Spec(ℤ)
  2. Arakelov intersection pairing (simplified for dimension 1)
  3. Arithmetic Chow group of Spec(ℤ̄)

  1. State the Arithmetic Hodge Index Theorem
  2. Reduce to Weil positivity (from Phase 3)
  3. Reduce Weil positivity to unitarity of scaling flow (from Phase 4)
  4. Reduce unitarity to Haar invariance (from Phase 4)
  5. Reduce Haar invariance to product formula (from Phase 4)
  6. The remaining sorry is: product formula + trace formula → positivity
     of the geometric side on autocorrelations.
  7. This is Workpacket 5. This is where you stop and document.

### 5.5 Directives for the Agent

1. **Always check Mathlib first.** Before building anything, search Mathlib
   docs and source for existing formalizations. Use `exact?`, `apply?`,
   `search_proof_tactic` liberally. Many lemmas exist under unexpected names.

2. **Build bottom-up.** Never attempt a later phase before completing all
   prerequisites. Each `sorry` in a prerequisite propagates uncertainty upward.

3. **Minimize sorry count.** Every sorry is a failure. Reduce them ruthlessly.
   If a sorry seems necessary, try three different proof strategies before
   accepting it. Document which strategies were tried and why they failed.

4. **Document every sorry.** Each sorry must have a comment block explaining:
   - What mathematical statement it represents
   - Why it cannot currently be proved in Lean
   - What would be needed to eliminate it (new Mathlib API? new math?)
   - An estimate of difficulty (routine / substantial / research-level)

5. **Test with #check and #eval.** After defining any structure, verify it
   type-checks. After stating any theorem, verify the type is `Prop`.
   Use `#check @your_theorem` liberally.

6. **Use the blueprint pattern.** For each major theorem, create a "blueprint"
   file that states the theorem and all its dependencies as a dependency graph.
   This allows multiple agents to work on different nodes independently.

7. **Prioritize Workpackets 1-4 over Workpacket 5.** Workpackets 1-4 are
   provable with known mathematics and sufficient Lean infrastructure.
   Workpacket 5 is research-level. Getting 1-4 done reduces the entire
   Riemann Hypothesis to a single, precisely-stated functional-analytic claim
   about trace formulas on adelic spaces. That reduction is itself a
   significant result.

8. **If you get stuck on infrastructure, build what you need.** If Stone's
   theorem requires an unbounded operator API that doesn't exist, build the
   API. If the Poisson summation formula requires a Schwartz space that isn't
   in Mathlib, define it. Every piece of infrastructure you build is
   independently valuable and can be PR'd to Mathlib.

9. **The physical intuition is your guide, not your proof.** The FEP / detailed
   balance framing tells you WHAT should be true and WHY. But every step must
   be proved formally. "Detailed balance implies positivity" is a theorem,
   not an axiom. Prove it or sorry it and document it.

10. **Record everything.** Every failed attempt is data. If a proof strategy
    doesn't work, document WHY it didn't work — what term couldn't be
    unified, what hypothesis was missing, what type mismatch occurred. This
    data is essential for future attempts.

### 5.6 Success Criteria

**Minimum viable output:** A Lean 4 project that:
- Compiles without errors
- States the Arithmetic Hodge Index Theorem as a formal Lean proposition
- Has ≤ 15 sorry's in the entire dependency chain
- Each sorry is documented with mathematical content and difficulty estimate
- Workpackets 1-3 are sorry-free (these are known mathematics)

**What this achieves:** The formal reduction of the Riemann Hypothesis to
a single, precisely-stated, atomic conjecture in functional analysis on
adelic spaces. Not a proof. But the transformation of a 167-year-old
mystery into a single, clear workpacket. That is what progress looks like.

## Appendix: The One-Sentence Summary

The Riemann Hypothesis is the statement that the integers, viewed as a
dissipative system maintaining the boundary between its own additive and
multiplicative structure, are at detailed balance — and the proof reduces
to showing that a perfect distributive law, which permits no information
leak across this boundary, forces the intersection pairing on the
arithmetic cohomology to be definite, which forces every fluctuation mode
of the prime distribution to have positive cost, which forces every zero
of the zeta function onto the unique axis of symmetry, which is the line
Re(s) = 1/2.
